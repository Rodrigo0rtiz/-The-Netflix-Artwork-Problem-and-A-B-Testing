01 · The Business Problem

Why Thumbnails Are Worth Millions

Netflix operates in one of the most attention-scarce environments in history. A subscriber opens the app and within seconds decides what to watch — or whether to watch at all. The thumbnail artwork attached to each title is often the only variable Netflix can control in that moment.

According to Netflix's own engineering blog, 82% of viewing decisions are influenced by the artwork shown on the home screen. A title with a poor thumbnail can underperform despite being critically acclaimed; a title with a compelling one can punch far above its weight.

This creates a concrete, high-stakes business question: how does Netflix know which artwork is better? The answer is systematic experimentation — specifically, A/B testing at massive scale.

"Netflix tests thousands of artwork variations simultaneously. Every thumbnail you see has survived a statistical competition against alternatives you never got to see."

Rather than relying on gut instinct or the opinion of a creative director, Netflix lets users vote with their clicks. Small groups of real subscribers are shown different artwork versions, behavior is measured, and the winner is rolled out globally. This is data-driven decision-making at industrial scale — and it is the core skill this project set out to simulate and understand.

02 · Experimentation & Machine Learning

The Engine Behind the Recommendation

A/B testing sits at the intersection of classical statistics and modern machine learning. At Netflix, these two disciplines work in tandem: machine learning models generate personalized artwork candidates for each user based on their viewing history, and A/B testing determines which of those candidates actually performs better in the real world.

01

Randomized Controlled Assignment

Users are randomly assigned to a control group (A) or treatment group (B). Random assignment is the foundation of valid causal inference — it ensures the two groups are statistically identical in every way except the artwork they see, so any difference in behavior can only be attributed to the artwork.

02

Metric Collection & Behavioral Logging

Every impression and click is logged as a timestamped event in Netflix's data warehouse. The primary metric for artwork experiments is click-through rate (CTR) — the fraction of users who clicked after seeing the artwork. This is a clean binary outcome: each user either clicked (1) or didn't (0).

03

Statistical Significance Testing

A difference in CTR between groups is not automatically meaningful. It could be random chance. Statistical testing quantifies exactly how likely the observed gap is to have occurred by luck alone. Only differences that clear a rigorous probability threshold are acted upon.

04

Ship Decision & Model Feedback

If the winning artwork clears the significance bar, it is deployed globally. The result also feeds back into Netflix's ML recommendation system, improving future artwork generation models for that genre, user segment, and content type.

03 · Project Methodology

What This Project Does

This project replicates the core statistical machinery of a Netflix artwork experiment using Python. The simulation spans three connected steps, each building on the last, and mirrors the actual workflow a Netflix data scientist would follow — from raw data generation through to a final business decision.

The Setup

Two artwork versions were defined: Artwork A (the control) with a true click probability of 0.24, and Artwork B (the treatment) with a true click probability of 0.30. In a real experiment, these probabilities are unknown — the experiment exists to discover them. Here, they are set in advance so the simulation has a known ground truth to verify against.

1,000 simulated users were randomly split 50/50 between the two groups. For each user, a single Bernoulli coin flip determined whether they clicked, weighted by their group's probability. The result was a clean, one-row-per-user DataFrame — the standard format for behavioral experiment data.

The Analysis

Group-level metrics were calculated from the raw data: total users, total clicks, and click-through rate per artwork version. These numbers were then visualized in a side-by-side bar chart with a relative lift annotation showing how much better B performed than A in percentage terms.

Finally, a two-proportion z-test was applied to determine whether the observed gap was statistically significant. The test produces a z-score, a p-value, and a 95% confidence interval — the three numbers that together answer the question: is this difference real, or could it be random noise?

Understanding the Statistical Test

The bar chart tells us Artwork B had a higher CTR. But higher by how much, and could it just be a coincidence? That's what the two-proportion z-test answers.

Think of it this way: if you flipped the same fair coin twice in a row and got different totals, you wouldn't conclude the coin changed — you'd chalk it up to chance. The z-test asks the same question about our two groups: is this 6-point gap bigger than what random variation alone would produce?

The test calculates a z-score — a single number representing how many standard deviations apart the two CTRs are. The higher the z-score, the more unusual the result would be if the two artworks were truly identical. Our z-score exceeds 2.5, which sits clearly in the red tail of the bell curve below.

Z-Test Distribution · Rejection Region Visualization

The p-value produced by the test is below 0.05 — the standard industry threshold. In plain English: if Artwork A and Artwork B were truly identical, there is less than a 5% chance we'd observe a gap this large just by random luck. That's a low enough probability to confidently conclude the difference is real.

The 95% confidence interval for the difference between the two CTRs does not include zero. This independently confirms the same conclusion: the true advantage of Artwork B over Artwork A is almost certainly positive, not a statistical mirage.

05 · The Code

Complete Python Implementation

The following code was written in Python and run in Google Colab. It is reproduced here in full for transparency. All three steps are combined into a single executable block.

netflix_ab_test.py 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.patches as mpatches
from scipy import stats
from scipy.stats import norm

# ── Experiment Parameters ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
np.random.seed(42)
n_users  = 1000
prob_A   = 0.24   # Click probability for Artwork A
prob_B   = 0.30   # Click probability for Artwork B

# ── STEP 1: Assign users & simulate clicks ─────────────────────────────────────
user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users, p=[0.5, 0.5])
clicks      = np.where(
    assignments == 'A',
    np.random.binomial(1, prob_A, size=n_users),
    np.random.binomial(1, prob_B, size=n_users)
)

df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id': user_ids,
    'artwork': assignments,
    'clicked': clicks
})

# ── STEP 2: Group-level metrics ────────────────────────────────────────────────
summary = df.groupby('artwork')['clicked'].agg(
    total_users  = 'count',
    total_clicks = 'sum',
    ctr          = 'mean'
).round(4)

n_A      = summary.loc['A', 'total_users']
n_B      = summary.loc['B', 'total_users']
clicks_A = summary.loc['A', 'total_clicks']
clicks_B = summary.loc['B', 'total_clicks']
ctr_A    = summary.loc['A', 'ctr']
ctr_B    = summary.loc['B', 'ctr']
lift     = (ctr_B - ctr_A) / ctr_A * 100

print("=" * 45)
print("        A/B TEST RESULTS SUMMARY")
print("=" * 45)
print(f"{'Metric':<22} {'Artwork A':>10} {'Artwork B':>10}")
print("-" * 45)
print(f"{'Total Users':<22} {n_A:>10} {n_B:>10}")
print(f"{'Total Clicks':<22} {clicks_A:>10} {clicks_B:>10}")
print(f"{'Click-Through Rate':<22} {ctr_A:>10.2%} {ctr_B:>10.2%}")
print("=" * 45)
print(f"\n  Relative lift of B over A: +{lift:.1f}%")
print("=" * 45)

# ── STEP 3: Two-Proportion Z-Test ─────────────────────────────────────────────
p_pool     = (clicks_A + clicks_B) / (n_A + n_B)
se         = np.sqrt(p_pool * (1 - p_pool) * (1/n_A + 1/n_B))
z_score    = (ctr_B - ctr_A) / se
p_value    = 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(abs(z_score)))
se_ci      = np.sqrt((ctr_A*(1-ctr_A)/n_A) + (ctr_B*(1-ctr_B)/n_B))
diff       = ctr_B - ctr_A
ci_lower   = diff - 1.96 * se_ci
ci_upper   = diff + 1.96 * se_ci
alpha      = 0.05
significant = p_value < alpha

print("\n" + "=" * 45)
print("       STATISTICAL TEST RESULTS")
print("=" * 45)
print(f"  {'Z-score:':<28} {z_score:.4f}")
print(f"  {'P-value:':<28} {p_value:.4f}")
print(f"  {'95% CI for difference:':<28} [{ci_lower:.4f}, {ci_upper:.4f}]")
if significant:
    print(f"  ✅ SIGNIFICANT — the difference is real.")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ NOT SIGNIFICANT — may be due to chance.")
print("=" * 45)

# ── STEP 4: Bar chart + Z-distribution ────────────────────────────────────────
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0F0F0F')

ax = axes[0]
ax.set_facecolor('#0F0F0F')
bars = ax.bar(['Artwork A', 'Artwork B'], [ctr_A, ctr_B],
              color=['#E50914', '#FF6B6B'], width=0.45, zorder=3)
for bar, val in zip(bars, [ctr_A, ctr_B]):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width()/2,
            bar.get_height() + 0.004, f'{val:.2%}',
            ha='center', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', color='white')

ax2 = axes[1]
ax2.set_facecolor('#0F0F0F')
x = np.linspace(-4.5, 4.5, 400)
y = norm.pdf(x)
ax2.plot(x, y, color='white', linewidth=2)
ax2.fill_between(x, y, where=(x <= -1.96), color='#E50914', alpha=0.6)
ax2.fill_between(x, y, where=(x >=  1.96), color='#E50914', alpha=0.6)
ax2.axvline(z_score, color='#FFD700', linewidth=2.2, linestyle='--',
            label=f'z = {z_score:.2f}')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('artwork_ab_test_full.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight',
            facecolor=fig.get_facecolor())
plt.show()
06 · Business Recommendation

What Netflix Should Do

The experiment produced a clear, statistically verified result. Here is what the data recommends, and the reasoning behind each point.

Ship Artwork B as the global default. The experiment showed that Artwork B generated a click-through rate approximately 6 percentage points higher than Artwork A — a relative improvement of roughly 25%. The two-proportion z-test confirmed this gap is statistically significant at the p < 0.05 level, meaning the probability that this result is due to random chance is less than 5%. The 95% confidence interval for the true difference does not include zero, providing independent confirmation. Netflix should retire Artwork A and deploy Artwork B to 100% of the user base immediately.

Before Shipping — Verify Downstream

CTR is a leading indicator, not the final word. Before a global rollout, Netflix should verify that the extra clicks driven by Artwork B translated into real engagement — specifically, that users who clicked actually watched a meaningful portion of the title. If Artwork B attracted curious clicks that immediately bounced, it may be misleading rather than improving the user experience.

Key downstream metrics to check: episode completion rate, hours watched per session, and 30-day retention for users who clicked through Artwork B versus Artwork A.

After Shipping — Feed the ML System

The results of this experiment shouldn't just inform one deployment decision. The features that made Artwork B more compelling — the specific visual elements, color palette, facial expressions, or compositional choices — should be fed back into Netflix's artwork generation ML models as positive signal.

Over time, this creates a reinforcement loop: better experiments train better models, which generate better artwork candidates, which produce cleaner experiments. This is how A/B testing becomes a compounding strategic asset, not just a one-off tactical tool.

Key Takeaway for Non-Technical Stakeholders

At Netflix's scale, a 25% improvement in CTR on a single title's artwork translates directly to more viewing hours, higher subscriber satisfaction, and reduced churn. Multiplied across thousands of titles and hundreds of millions of users, systematic A/B testing is not a data science nicety — it is a core revenue driver. This project demonstrates exactly the kind of disciplined, evidence-based thinking that separates high-performing product organizations from those that guess.
